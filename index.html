What Problem Does TabICL Solve?

Every time a bank wants to predict which customers will default, the standard process is the same: collect labelled data, train a model (XGBoost, neural net), tune hyperparameters, validate, and deploy. This takes weeks — and must be repeated for every new dataset.

TabICL asks: can we pre-train one model that learns the general skill of reading any table and predicting any target, so that on a new dataset, no training is needed at all?

🧠 The Core Idea

TabICL is pre-trained on ~82 million synthetic tables it generated itself. From this it learns a transferable skill: given a table of labelled rows plus one unlabelled row, predict the label. At inference time, your real data is just "context." The model reads it and predicts in a single forward pass — zero gradient updates on your data.

Throughout this guide we use a running example from Amex Default Prediction. Five features. The target is whether a customer defaults.

§ 01

Understanding Attention: Q, K, V — For First-Time Readers

Before diving into TabICL, you need to understand one idea: attention. It is the core operation in all modern AI — GPT, BERT, and TabICL. The idea is surprisingly intuitive.

The Job Interview Analogy

Imagine you are a hiring manager looking for a "senior data scientist with Python and finance experience." You have 100 CVs on your desk. You do three things:

// Step-by-step: What Q, K, V mean in human terms
❓
Q
Query — "What am I looking for?"

Your job description: "senior data scientist, Python, finance." This is what you need. In TabICL, the Query is the test customer C_005 saying: "I have high balance, 19 days delinquent, low payment ratio — who among the training customers is most like me?"

🏷️
K
Key — "What does each candidate advertise?"

The headline on each CV: "5 years Python, ML, banking." This is what each item says it contains. In TabICL, each training customer's Key summarises their profile — "high balance, 22 days late" — so C_005 can judge how similar they are.

📄
V
Value — "What information does each candidate actually carry?"

The full CV content: actual work history, skills, projects. Once you find the best-matching candidates (high Q·K score), you read their Values to form your decision. In TabICL, the Value is the training customer's label signal — "this person defaulted" — which C_005 borrows to form its prediction.

The output = a weighted average of all Values, where the weights come from how well each Key matched the Query. You do not just pick the single best CV — you form a judgment that is a blend, heavily influenced by the best matches.

The Math — Kept to One Formula

The Attention Formula
Step 1 — How well does my query match each key? score(i, j) = Q_i · K_j / √d_k Q_i = query vector of item i (what am I looking for?) K_j = key vector of item j (what does j advertise?) · = dot product — bigger when the two vectors point in the same direction (i.e., are similar) √d_k = scaling factor to prevent scores from getting too large Step 2 — Convert scores to weights that sum to 1: α_j = softmax(score(i,j)) = exp(score(i,j)) / Σ_all_k exp(score(i,k)) Bigger score → bigger weight. All weights sum to 1. This is "how much do I pay attention to item j?" Step 3 — Weighted blend of Values: output_i = Σ_j α_j × V_j α_j = attention weight for item j V_j = value vector of item j Output = information blended from all items, weighted by relevance to my query.
Nothing magical here. Q·K is just measuring alignment (like a similarity score). Softmax turns scores into weights. The final output is a weighted average. The "learning" happens because Q, K, V are all produced by learned weight matrices W_Q, W_K, W_V — the model learns what to look for (Q), how to advertise (K), and what information to pass (V).

Data: How Independent (Input) Features Are Generated

TabICL never trains on real Amex data. Instead it trains on synthetic tables it manufactures itself — 82 million of them. Each table is built using a process called a Structural Causal Model (SCM). Think of it as a recipe for building a realistic-looking dataset from random ingredients.

The Big Picture: A Causal Graph (DAG)

Real-world variables cause each other. In Amex data: high spending causes high balance, which causes late payments, which causes default risk. The SCM simulates this chain of causation using a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) — a flowchart with one-way arrows and no loops.

Definition 2.2 — The Tree-Based SCM in Detail

Definition 2.2 — Tree-Based SCM: Step by Step
Goal: produce x_c = f(x_{Pa(c)}) where f looks like a tree model. Given: parent values X_parents ∈ R^{n × p} (n customers, p parent features) Step 1 — Sample random "fake" targets: Y_fake ~ N(0, I) ∈ R^{n × q} (q random columns of Gaussian noise — the "targets" are meaningless) Step 2 — Fit an XGBoost model: XGB_model = XGBoostRegressor(X_parents → Y_fake) (XGBoost tries to predict the noise using the parent values) Step 3 — Use its predictions as the child's values: x_c = XGB_model.predict(X_parents) Why does fitting noise produce useful structure? XGBoost builds decision trees over X_parents. Even when predicting noise, those trees carve up the feature space into regions using thresholds — e.g., "if Income > 0.6 AND Employment > 0.4, predict +0.8." Those threshold-based splits ARE the structure we want. The targets are noise, but the structural form of f is real. Model parameters sampled per node: n_estimators ~ min(4, 1 + Exp(λ=0.5)) max_depth ~ min(4, 2 + Exp(λ=0.5))
This is subtle but important. We are not training XGBoost to do something useful — we are using it as a random function generator that produces tree-shaped (piecewise constant) mappings from parent values to child values. The noise targets ensure the mapping is random; the XGBoost structure ensures it has the threshold-like shape common in real financial data.

What Are the Final Features? Are They All Child Nodes?

Not necessarily. The final feature set includes all non-target nodes — which can be a mix of root and child nodes. In practice, the SCM is structured so that most observable features are child nodes (they depend on at least one parent). Root nodes are usually latent variables that are never directly observed — they just seed the causal chain.

Dependent Features Within X — Correlations Are Intentional

Because child nodes are computed from parents, the features in the final table are correlated — just like real data. Balance Ratio and Risk Score both descend from Spending, so they tend to move together. This is by design: TabICL needs to learn to handle correlated, dependent features, not just independent ones.

💡 Layman Summary: Why "Dependent" Features?

In real financial data, a customer who spends a lot tends to also have a high balance AND a high risk score — because all three trace back to the same root cause (their spending behaviour). The SCM bakes this dependency structure in from the start. A model trained on independent random features would fail on real data. TabICL trains on correlated features that look like real datasets — so it handles them naturally at inference time.

§ 03

Data: How the Target Variable (Label) Is Generated

Once all feature columns are built via the SCM, we need a label column — Default or No Default. In real data this is observed. In pre-training data, we manufacture it from the SCM outputs.

The Simple Idea: Cut a Continuous Score Into Buckets

One child node (call it z) is designated as the "latent risk score." It is a continuous number. We then cut it at percentile thresholds — customers below the 30th percentile of z are labelled Default, the rest No Default. In layman terms: rank all 1000 customers by their hidden risk score. Label the bottom 300 as "Default." Done.

The number of classes C is randomly chosen between 2 and 10 per synthetic dataset — so TabICL sees binary, 3-class, and 10-class problems during training and handles all of them at inference time.

§ 04

Stage 1: Column Embedding — Reading Each Feature in Context (TFcol)

Raw numbers mean nothing without context. The value 19 in "Days Delinquent" is alarming if most customers have 0 days — but unremarkable if most customers have 20+. Stage 1 reads the entire column before interpreting any single cell.

💡 The Loan Officer Analogy

Before judging any single customer's "Days Delinquent = 19," an experienced loan officer flips through all 1000 files first, building a mental picture: "Most customers have 0 days. A few have 5–15. Nineteen is pretty extreme here." Only then do they assess that specific customer. Stage 1 does this automatically for every column, for every dataset.

What Is ISAB? — The Efficient Column Reader

Stage 1 uses something called an Induced Self-Attention Block (ISAB). The problem it solves: standard attention compares every customer against every other customer, costing O(n²) — 10 billion operations for 100K customers. Definition 4.1 — Linear Projection: Turning a Number Into a Vector

Before ISAB can do anything, each raw scalar cell value (a single number like 19) must become a vector of 128 numbers. Transformers only speak in vectors, not scalars. The linear projection does this conversion:

Definition 4.1 — Linear Projection: From One Number to 128 Numbers
The problem: the transformer needs a vector input, but each cell is a scalar. c_{ij} = 19 (Days Delinquent for customer i) ↑ just one number The solution — multiply by a weight vector w and add a bias b: U[i] = Lin(c_{ij}) = w × c_{ij} + b ∈ R^{128} w ∈ R^{128} = a learnable weight vector (128 numbers, one per output dim) b ∈ R^{128} = a learnable bias vector (128 numbers, one per output dim) × = scalar multiplication (just multiply each element of w by 19) Tiny example with d=3 instead of 128: w = [0.2, −0.5, 0.8] b = [0.1, 0.3, −0.2] c = 19 U[i] = 19 × [0.2, −0.5, 0.8] + [0.1, 0.3, −0.2] = [3.8, −9.5, 15.2] + [0.1, 0.3, −0.2] = [3.9, −9.2, 15.0] ← a 3-dim (or 128-dim) vector Why is this useful? The same w and b are shared across ALL columns and ALL datasets. This makes the model transferable — it learns a universal "how to turn any number into a vector" mapping during pre-training. A value of 19 in "Days Delinquent" and 19 in "Risk Score" get the same initial vector — but ISAB will differentiate them using the column's distribution context in the next step.
The weights w and b are learned during pre-training. After 82 million synthetic tables, they have learned to project scalar values into a space where the ISAB can efficiently detect distributional patterns (median, outliers, skewness) from the distribution of projected values within a column.

Stage 2: Row Pooling — Building a Portrait of Each Customer (TFrow)

After Stage 1, we have a context-aware embedding for every cell — 5 features × 5 customers = 25 embedding vectors (each 128-dim). But Stage 3 needs to compare whole customers against each other, not individual cells. Stage 2 merges each customer's 5 feature-embeddings into one single summary vector.

💡 The Summary Paragraph Analogy

Each customer is a 5-sentence document (one sentence per feature). After Stage 1, each sentence has a rich, context-aware meaning. Stage 2 reads all 5 sentences and writes a one-paragraph summary that captures how the sentences interact: "High balance AND high days delinquent AND low payments together signal a distressed customer." The [CLS] token is that summary paragraph.

The Representation Collapse Problem

A naïve approach — just run a transformer over each row's features — fails in one important case. If two columns have identical statistical distributions, the model cannot tell them apart without position information. It produces the same embedding for two different customers who just have their feature values swapped across columns.

// Illustration: Collapse — Two Different Customers Get the Same Embedding

"some value is 0.9, some is 0.1, some is 0.5"
It doesn't know which is which.

Balance=0.1  Spend=0.9  Pay=0.5

Customer A has high balance and low spending — very different risk profile from B (low balance, high spending). But without column position awareness, the transformer sees the same set of numbers and produces identical embeddings. This is representation collapse.

The Fix: RoPE — Stamping Each Column with a Unique Angle

RoPE (Rotary Positional Embedding) solves this by rotating the attention query and key vectors by an angle that depends on the column's position index. Column 1 gets rotated by one angle. Column 2 by a different angle. Even if their raw values are identical, the rotated vectors point in different directions — so the attention mechanism can distinguish them.

💡 The Clock Hand Analogy

Imagine each column position as a clock hand pointing in a unique direction: Balance points at 12 o'clock, Spend at 2 o'clock, Payment at 4 o'clock, Days at 7 o'clock, Risk at 10 o'clock. When the transformer computes similarity between two feature embeddings, it automatically accounts for how far apart their clock hands are. Features at different positions "feel" different — even if their raw values are the same number.

Definition 5.1 — How RoPE Is Added to Each Embedding Vector
We have feature embedding e_{i,j} ∈ R^{128} for customer i, column j. RoPE modifies this embedding BEFORE it enters the attention mechanism. ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ STEP 1 — Compute rotation angles for column position j ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ For each pair of embedding dimensions (2i, 2i+1), i = 0,1,...,63: θᵢ = j / (100000^(2i / 128)) j = column index (1 for Balance, 2 for Spend, etc.) 100000 = large base constant — controls how fast angles grow 2i/128 = fraction that slows down rotation for high-dimension pairs Concrete example for column j=1 (Balance) vs j=4 (Days): i=0 pair: θ_Balance = 1/1 = 1.0 rad ≈ 57° θ_Days = 4/1 = 4.0 rad ≈ 229° i=63 pair: θ_Balance = 1/100000^(1) ≈ 0.00001 rad (nearly zero) θ_Days = 4/100000^(1) ≈ 0.00004 rad (still tiny) Early dimensions rotate a LOT with column index → encode fine position. The Value vector V is NOT rotated — only Q and K. ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ STEP 3 — What does rotating Q and K achieve? ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ The attention score between column j and column k becomes: Q'_j · K'_k = f(Q_j, K_k, |j−k|) It now depends on the RELATIVE DISTANCE |j−k| between columns. Balance (j=1) and Spend (j=2) are adjacent → small distance → can still interact strongly. Balance (j=1) and Risk (j=5) are far apart → larger distance → naturally weaker attention. Most importantly: column 2 and column 4, even with identical raw values, produce Q' vectors that point in different directions (rotated by different angles) → their dot products differ → the model can distinguish them. Why base=100,000? Small angles for large j values → smooth generalisation to more columns than seen during training (tested up to 200 features despite training on max 100). Large base = wide range.
RoPE is applied to Q and K only — never V. This is intentional: V carries the actual information to be passed forward. Rotating V would distort the information content. Rotating Q and K only changes how similarity is computed (which features pay attention to which), not what information flows once attention weights are determined.

Why keep only CLS, not all feature vectors?
The 5 feature token outputs (e_{i,1}...e_{i,5}) each describe one specific feature. They are column-specific. The CLS tokens are column-agnostic — they have no assigned column, so they are free to encode cross-feature interactions holistically. The model learns during pre-training to pack the most prediction-relevant information into the CLS slots. Keeping all 9 output vectors (4 CLS + 5 features) would also give a much larger vector (9×128=1152 dims) for Stage 3 to process — making the n×n attention in Stage 3 more expensive. The 4 CLS design is a deliberate compression: distill 9 vectors into 4 summary slots.

Stage 3: In-Context Prediction — How h_i Vectors Attend to Each Other (TFicl)

We now have H = [h_1, ..., h_n] — one 512-dim portrait vector per customer. Training customers also carry their label (fused in via E_label). The test customer C_005 carries no label. Stage 3 runs a 12-layer transformer over all these h_i vectors simultaneously — letting every row attend to every other row. This is where prediction happens.

🧠 The Core Operation

Imagine seating all 5 customers around a table. Each one looks around at every other person and asks: "How similar is your profile to mine?" Training customers can answer with their label. C_005 listens to all answers, weights them by similarity, and forms its prediction. That is exactly what the self-attention inside TF_icl does — but in 512-dimensional space, with learned similarity measures, and across 12 progressively refined rounds.

All n row vectors (H_train and H_test combined) are now fed into TF_icl — a 12-layer transformer. At each layer, every row vector computes its own Q, K, V and attends to every other row. Here is the complete operation — with every symbol explained.

Definition 6.2 — Full Stage 3 Self-Attention Across All h_i Vectors
Input: H_input = [H_train[1], ..., H_train[n_tr], H_test[1], ..., H_test[n_te]] ∈ R^{n × 512} n = n_tr + n_te total rows ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ FOR EVERY ROW i (train and test alike): ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ Q_i = W_Q · H_input[i] ∈ R^{512} K_i = W_K · H_input[i] ∈ R^{512} V_i = W_V · H_input[i] ∈ R^{512} W_Q, W_K, W_V ∈ R^{512×512} — learned weight matrices. All three project the SAME 512-dim input into different roles: Q_i = "what am I looking for in other rows?" K_i = "what do I advertise about myself?" (V_i contains both feature AND label signal, since H_input[i] already fused both) ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ SIMILARITY SCORE (row i attending to row j): ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ score(i, j) = Q_i · K_j / √512 Q_i · K_j = dot product = a single number measuring how aligned i's "question" is with j's "answer" High value → i and j are similar profiles Low value → i and j are dissimilar √512 = scaling factor to prevent scores from growing so large that softmax becomes a hard one-hot ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ MASKING — BLOCKING ILLEGAL ATTENTION PATHS: ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ Mask matrix M ∈ R^{n × n}: M[i, j] = 0 if j is a TRAINING row → attend freely M[i, j] = −∞ if j is a TEST row → completely blocked Why −∞? Because softmax(−∞) = exp(−∞)/Σ = 0/Σ = 0. Any row that attends to a test-row position gets weight exactly zero from that position. It is as if that position does not exist. ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ ATTENTION WEIGHTS (for row C_005): ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ α_j = softmax( score(C005, j) + M[C005, j] ) = exp(score + M[j]) / Σ_k exp(score + M[k]) Result for our Amex example: α_{C001} = 0.41 (similar: high balance, late payments) α_{C002} = 0.03 (dissimilar: low balance, on-time) α_{C003} = 0.49 (most similar: high balance, 22d late) α_{C004} = 0.07 (somewhat dissimilar: moderate profile) Σ = 1.00 ✓ ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ OUTPUT VECTOR FOR C_005: ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ o_{C005} = Σ_j α_j × V_j ∈ R^{512} = 0.41 × V_{C001} ← carries Default signal (high weight) + 0.03 × V_{C002} ← carries NoDefault signal (low weight) + 0.49 × V_{C003} ← carries Default signal (highest weight) + 0.07 × V_{C004} ← carries NoDefault signal (low weight) → 90% of the blended output comes from Default-labelled rows → o_{C005} is dominated by the "Default" signal
This runs 12 times (12 attention layers). Each layer refines the h_i vectors — early layers establish rough similarity, later layers integrate subtler cross-row interactions. By layer 12, each test row's output vector is a rich blend of information from the most relevant training rows.

Deep Dive: How Masking Works — And Why It Is Essential

The masking step is the single most important safety mechanism in Stage 3. Without it, the model could "cheat" by copying label information across test rows. Here is exactly how it works and what would go wrong without it.

// The n×n Attention Matrix — What Gets Masked and Why
Row i attends to → C_001
Training		 C_002
Training		 C_003
Training		 C_004
Training		 C_005
Test
C_001 (Train) ✓ 0 ✓ 0 ✓ 0 ✓ 0 ✗ −∞
C_002 (Train) ✓ 0 ✓ 0 ✓ 0 ✓ 0 ✗ −∞
C_003 (Train) ✓ 0 ✓ 0 ✓ 0 ✓ 0 ✗ −∞
C_005 (Test) ✓ 0 ✓ 0 ✓ 0 ✓ 0 ✗ −∞
✓ M[i,j] = 0 — ALLOWED (adds nothing to score)
All rows can freely attend to all training rows. Training rows carry label signal (via E_label). This is exactly where the prediction information comes from.
✗ M[i,j] = −∞ — BLOCKED (softmax → weight 0)
No row can attend to any test row. Test rows carry no label — attending to them would spread noise, not signal. The −∞ makes softmax output exactly 0 for that position.
Every row — training and test alike — is blocked from attending to test rows. Even C_001 cannot attend to C_005. This prevents any cross-contamination between test rows and ensures all label signal flows only from training → test, never in the other direction. Notice every row also blocks attending to itself via the diagonal (standard in transformers to prevent trivial self-copying).
⚠️ Why Masking Is Critical — The Cheating Scenario

Suppose we had two test rows C_005 and C_006. Without masking, C_005 could attend to C_006 and vice versa. But neither carries a label. They would just exchange their own portrait information — noise circulating between two unknown rows. Worse, if the model learned to use this cross-test attention during training, it would be exploiting patterns across test rows that would not be present at real inference time (when test rows are scored independently). Masking to −∞ eliminates this entirely: test rows are read-only observers of training rows.

Model Training: How the Weights Are Learned

All parameters — the ISAB weights in Stage 1, the CLS tokens and RoPE in Stage 2, the Q/K/V matrices and label embeddings in Stage 3, the MLP head — are learned during pre-training, entirely on synthetic data. No real Amex data is ever involved.

The Training Loop

1
Generate a completely fresh synthetic table

A new random DAG is sampled. Features generated via SCM. Target created via quantile discretisation. This table has never been seen before and will never be seen again — n rows, m features, C classes all randomly chosen.

2
Split into training rows and test rows (80/20)

80% become "context" — their labels are visible to the model. 20% become test rows — labels hidden. This mirrors exactly what happens at inference time on real data.

3
Run the full forward pass: Stage 1 → Stage 2 → Stage 3

Output: predicted probabilities for every test row. No label information about test rows was used.

4
Compute the cross-entropy loss

L = −(1/n_te) Σ log p̂(y_true | x_test). Compare predictions to the true hidden labels. Lower loss = better predictions.

5
Backpropagate and update all weights

Compute how much each parameter contributed to the error. Nudge every parameter slightly to reduce it. Repeat 162,050 times across all 3 stages.

🧠 The Meta-Learning Key

Every step uses a completely different dataset — discarded after one use. The only signal that reduces loss across all possible synthetic datasets is learning to genuinely read tabular structure. The model cannot memorise any specific table. It must develop the abstract skill of in-context prediction. That skill, encoded in the weights, transfers to your real Amex data.

Curriculum Learning — Why Three Stages and What Happens in Each

You would not teach calculus before arithmetic. TabICL is trained on small tables first, then larger ones. Here is exactly what happens in each stage, why each design choice was made, and what the result is.

STAGE 1 — Foundation
160,000 steps · n=1,024 rows (fixed) · Batch N_B=4 tables per step

What exactly happens at each step: Four fresh synthetic tables are generated (each with exactly 1,024 rows). All four are run through Stages 1→2→3 in parallel (batched). The combined cross-entropy loss across all four is computed. Backpropagation updates every weight in all three stages simultaneously. This repeats 160,000 times — each time with four new tables no one has ever seen before.

Why n=1,024? Small enough that Stage 3's O(n²) attention fits easily in GPU memory (1024² ≈ 1 million operations — very fast). Large enough that the model sees enough labelled context to learn non-trivial patterns. At this scale, all three stages learn their basics: Stage 1 learns to read column distributions, Stage 2 learns to build customer portraits, Stage 3 learns basic in-context lookup.

The learning rate schedule — cosine decay with restarts: The learning rate starts high (1×10⁻⁴), drops smoothly following a cosine curve to near zero, then resets (restarts) to high again. This happens multiple times over 160,000 steps. Each restart gives the model a chance to escape shallow local minima and explore different parameter regions before settling. Think of it as: study hard → rest → study hard again → rest, gradually consolidating knowledge.

Result after Stage 1: TabICL ranks 9th out of 30+ methods on the TALENT benchmark. It has learned the fundamentals but cannot handle datasets larger than ~1,024 rows — it has never seen them during training.

STAGE 2 — Scaling Up
2,000 steps · n ~ LogUniform(1K, 40K) · Batch N_B=1 table per step

What exactly happens at each step: A dataset size n is randomly drawn from LogUniform(1K, 40K) — meaning 1K, 5K, 10K, and 40K tables are all equally likely on a log scale. One synthetic table of that size is generated. It is run through Stages 1→2→3. Loss is computed, backpropagation updates all weights.

Why LogUniform and not Uniform? Uniform(1K, 40K) would mean most tables have ~20K rows and very few have 1K rows. LogUniform ensures small tables (1K–5K) and large tables (20K–40K) are seen with equal frequency. The model must remain competent at both small and large contexts simultaneously — not just large ones.

The memory problem at large n — Activation Checkpointing: For n > 10,000 rows, Stage 3's attention matrix is n×n — at n=40K, that is 1.6 billion numbers, far too large to keep in GPU memory during backpropagation. The normal backprop approach stores all intermediate values (activations) so gradients can be computed on the backward pass. Activation checkpointing discards those intermediate values during the forward pass and recomputes them on-the-fly during the backward pass. This trades extra computation (~33% more time) for a ~10× reduction in memory. It is why N_B=1 instead of 4 at this stage — only one large table fits in memory at a time.

Why reduce the number of features at large n? At n=40K rows and m=100 features, Stage 2's O(n·m²) complexity becomes enormous. The number of features m is capped at smaller values for large n so that the total computation remains feasible.

The learning rate schedule — polynomial decay: Starts at η=2×10⁻⁵ (much lower than Stage 1 — the model is already well-trained, no need for large steps). Decays following (1 − t/T)² to η=5×10⁻⁶ over 2,000 steps. No restarts — the model makes steady, gentle progress toward adapting to variable dataset sizes.

Result after Stage 2: TabICL jumps to 2nd place on the benchmark, tied with TabPFNv2. A dramatic improvement from learning to handle variable dataset sizes. The model now works well from 1K to 40K rows.

STAGE 3 — Large-Table Specialisation
50 steps · n ~ Uniform(40K, 60K) · Only TF_icl trained · Stages 1 & 2 frozen

What exactly happens at each step: A table of 40K–60K rows is generated. Stages 1 and 2 do not receive any gradient updates — their weights are frozen (fixed). Only Stage 3's TF_icl weights are updated. This is because at 60K rows, Stage 3's n×n attention alone uses almost all available GPU memory. Running backpropagation through Stages 1 and 2 as well would require storing their intermediate activations — there simply is not enough memory.

Why freeze Stages 1 and 2, and is that safe? Stages 1 and 2 process data column-by-column and row-by-row respectively — their complexity does not depend on n in the same way. They are already well-trained after Stage 2 (which included tables up to 40K rows). The only component that needs to adapt to 40K–60K row contexts is Stage 3's in-context attention mechanism — specifically, learning to attend meaningfully when there are 50,000 labelled training rows instead of 1,000. Freezing the foundation and only fine-tuning the top is a standard technique (analogous to feature extraction in computer vision).

Why only 50 steps? Stage 3 weights are initialised from Stage 2 — which already saw tables up to 40K rows. The jump from 40K to 60K is incremental, not dramatic. Only the largest-context attention patterns need minor adjustment. 50 steps × 512 datasets = 25,600 large-table examples is enough without overfitting or forgetting Stage 2 gains.

Learning rate: constant at 2×10⁻⁶ — very small and stable. No decay, no restarts. We want minimal, targeted updates to Stage 3 only.

Result after Stage 3: TabICL reaches 1st place overall. It now handles 1K to 60K rows during training and generalises to 500K rows at inference (via the memory-efficient batched inference pipeline).

§ 10

Key Assumptions

ASSUMPTION 01
IID Rows Within a Dataset

All rows are assumed independently drawn from the same distribution. No time-series dependencies or sequential structure. Customer behaviour over time is ignored — each row is treated as a standalone snapshot.

ASSUMPTION 02
Train and Test from Same Distribution

Training and test rows are assumed to come from the same joint distribution P(X, y). Distribution shift across time periods, geographies, or product lines degrades performance — just like any model.

ASSUMPTION 03
Target Genuinely Depends on Features

Pre-training filtered out datasets where y is independent of X. TabICL expects labels to depend on the features. If your features have near-zero predictive power, outputs will be unreliable.

ASSUMPTION 04
Primarily Numeric Features

Designed for numeric inputs. Categorical features require ordinal encoding (0, 1, 2...), which imposes an artificial ordering. High-cardinality categoricals are out of scope.

ASSUMPTION 05
Classification Target ≤ 10 Classes

The base model handles up to 10 discrete classes. Regression is not natively supported. More than 10 classes uses hierarchical decomposition — each level is solved independently, introducing approximation error.

ASSUMPTION 06
Column Ordering Approximately Arbitrary

RoPE makes the model slightly column-order sensitive. Mitigated by ensembling over 32 random column permutations, but perfect permutation invariance is not guaranteed.

Option 1: Build From Scratch

⚠️ Critical Clarification — Can I Train TabICL From Scratch on Amex Data?

No — and you would not want to. "Build from scratch" in this context means training a conventional model (XGBoost, LightGBM, a custom neural net) entirely on your labelled Amex data. It does not mean replicating TabICL's pre-training on Amex data.

Here is why training TabICL from scratch on Amex is neither feasible nor useful:
(1) TabICL's architecture is designed for in-context learning — not for being trained on a fixed dataset. If you removed the synthetic pre-training and tried to train TabICL on one real dataset, the ICL mechanism would have nothing to generalise from. It would be like taking a GPT-4 architecture and training it on one book — the model would overfit trivially with no meaningful capability.
(2) The pre-training costs 20 GPU-days and 82 million synthetic tables. Reproducing it requires the SCM data generator, the full training pipeline, and significant GPU compute. It is open-source — technically possible — but only if you want to change the pre-training prior or architecture, not to train on Amex data.
"Build from scratch" = XGBoost or similar, trained on your Amex labels, no TabICL involved.

The Core Challenge: Catastrophic Forgetting

When you update TabICL's weights on only Amex data, it tends to forget everything it learned about other table types. Two techniques address this:

⚓
A
L2-SP Regularisation — Stay Close to Pre-trained Weights

Add a penalty to the loss: L(θ) = L_task(θ) + λ·||θ − θ₀||². This penalises large deviations from the original pre-trained weights θ₀. Large λ = preserve general ability. Small λ = risk catastrophic forgetting. Typical: λ = 0.01–0.1.

🪶
B
LoRA — Low-Rank Adaptation (Preferred)

Instead of updating all weights, add small adapter matrices A and B to the Q, K, V matrices: W_new = W₀ + A·B, where A ∈ R^{512×8}, B ∈ R^{8×512}. Only A and B are trained — 99.7% fewer parameters. W₀ stays frozen, preserving the pre-trained structure. The adapter learns what is Amex-specific on top of the general foundation.

✓ Pros
  • Best of both worlds at moderate data (1K–50K rows): better than scratch (faster convergence, needs less data), better than direct (adapts to Amex-specific patterns)
  • LoRA: tiny parameter footprint — easy to maintain per-portfolio adapters
  • Particularly strong for new products where labelled data is scarce
✗ Cons
  • Not officially supported — requires custom training code
  • Risk of catastrophic forgetting without careful regularisation
  • Requires GPU for training
  • Model governance challenge: combining pre-trained synthetic weights with real data complicates model risk documentation
📌 When to Choose

1K–50K labelled rows, domain-specific patterns unlikely in synthetic data, GPU access, ML engineers comfortable with transformer training.

  • Black box — attention weights are hard to translate into regulatory-friendly explanations
  • Above 60K training rows requires subsampling, discarding information

💡 The Practical Recommendation

For most organisations exploring TabICL: start with Option 3. It takes hours, requires no ML infrastructure beyond a GPU, and gives you a strong baseline. Compare it against your existing XGBoost model on a held-out test set. If it is within 2–3% on your key metric (AUC, log-loss), the operational simplicity wins. If you have abundant data and a regulated environment demanding interpretability and millisecond latency, invest in Option 1. Option 2 is the emerging frontier — watch for official TabICL fine-tuning support in future releases.
